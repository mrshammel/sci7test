Introduction to Ecosystems

Discover the living and non-living parts of ecosystems and how they work together.

STS-K.1STS-K.2

🎯 What You'll Learn

  • Define what an ecosystem is and identify examples
  • Distinguish between biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) factors
  • Explain how biotic and abiotic factors interact and depend on each other
  • Describe how ecosystems meet the needs of living things

🌎 What Is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (plants, animals, fungi, bacteria) interacting with each other and their non-living environment (water, air, soil, sunlight). Ecosystems can be as small as a puddle or as large as the entire Earth!

Every ecosystem has two main types of components working together:

🌱

Biotic Factors

All living or once-living things: plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, and their remains

☀️

Abiotic Factors

All non-living elements: sunlight, water, temperature, soil, air, minerals

💡 Key Idea: Every ecosystem depends on the interactions between biotic and abiotic factors. Remove one piece, and the entire system can be affected!

🔬 Biotic Factors: The Living World

Biotic factors include every living organism in an ecosystem — from the tallest tree to the tiniest bacterium. They can be grouped into:

🌳

Plants

Produce food through photosynthesis; provide oxygen and shelter

🦌

Animals

Consume plants or other animals; help with pollination and seed dispersal

🍄

Fungi

Decompose dead matter; recycle nutrients back into the soil

🦠

Bacteria

Break down organic materials; some fix nitrogen for plant use

Dead organisms are still classified as biotic because they were once living. Fallen leaves, dead animals, and decaying wood all still play crucial roles in the ecosystem — decomposers break them down, returning nutrients to the soil that living plants can use. The distinction is about origin, not current state!

💧 Abiotic Factors: The Non-Living World

Abiotic factors are the non-living parts of an ecosystem. Even though they aren't alive, they are essential for the survival of every organism.

Abiotic Factor Role in the Ecosystem
☀️ Sunlight Energy source for photosynthesis; affects temperature and seasons
💧 Water Essential for all life; habitat for aquatic organisms
🌡️ Temperature Determines which species can survive in an area
🪨 Soil & Minerals Provides nutrients for plants; habitat for organisms like worms
💨 Air (Atmosphere) Provides oxygen for animals and CO₂ for plants
🌧️ Precipitation Delivers water to land ecosystems; shapes landscapes

🔗 How Ecosystems Meet the Needs of Life

Every living organism needs certain things to survive. A healthy ecosystem provides all of these:

🥗

Nutrients

Food and minerals for growth, energy, and repair

⚡

Energy

Sunlight drives photosynthesis; energy flows through food chains

💦

Moisture

Water for cellular processes, temperature regulation

🏠

Habitat

Shelter, space, and suitable conditions for living and reproducing

🌬️

Gas Exchange

O₂ for animals, CO₂ for plants — a constant cycle

Lesson 2

Food Chains, Food Webs & Energy Flow

Trace the flow of energy and nutrients through an ecosystem.

STS-K.2

🎯 What You'll Learn

  • Identify producers, consumers, and decomposers in an ecosystem
  • Explain how energy flows through a food chain
  • Describe how food chains connect to form food webs
  • Understand energy pyramids and why energy decreases at each level
  • Predict what happens when a link in a food chain is disrupted

⚡ Energy in Ecosystems

All life needs energy to survive. In nearly every ecosystem on Earth, that energy begins with the Sun. But how does energy get from sunlight to a wolf? Through a series of steps called a food chain!

Energy flows in one direction: from the Sun → to producers → to consumers → and is recycled by decomposers.

💡 Key Idea: Energy is not recycled — it flows one way and is lost as heat at each step. Only about 10% of energy is passed from one level to the next!

🌱 Producers, Consumers & Decomposers

Every organism in an ecosystem plays a role based on how it gets its energy:

🌿

Producers

Autotrophs — make their own food through photosynthesis. Examples: grass, trees, algae

🐰

Primary Consumers

Herbivores — eat producers. Examples: rabbits, deer, grasshoppers

🐍

Secondary Consumers

Carnivores/Omnivores — eat primary consumers. Examples: snakes, frogs

🦅

Tertiary Consumers

Top predators — eat secondary consumers. Examples: eagles, wolves, sharks

🍄

Decomposers

Break down dead organisms and waste, returning nutrients to soil. Examples: fungi, bacteria

Scavengers (like vultures and hyenas) eat dead animals but don't break them down completely. Decomposers (like bacteria and fungi) break down dead matter at the molecular level, returning nutrients directly to the soil.

🔗 Food Chains

A food chain is a simple, straight-line path showing who eats whom. Each step is called a trophic level.

🌞 Sun → 🌱 Grass (producer) → 🐰 Rabbit (primary consumer) → 🐍 Snake (secondary consumer) → 🦅 Hawk (tertiary consumer) → 🍄 Decomposers

The arrows in a food chain show the direction that energy flows.

🕸️ Food Webs

In reality, most organisms eat more than one thing and are eaten by more than one predator. A food web shows all of these interconnected food chains.

Removing one species can have a ripple effect throughout the entire food web! This is called a trophic cascade. The more interconnected a food web is, the more stable the ecosystem tends to be.

Lesson 3

Populations & Communities

How populations grow, what limits them, and what carrying capacity means for ecosystems.

👥 What Is a Population?

A population is a group of organisms of the same species living together in a specific area. A population of deer in a forest, a school of fish in a lake — these are all populations.

📏

Population Size

The total number of individuals in a population at a given time.

📊

Population Density

How crowded a population is — the number of organisms per unit area.

🗺️

Distribution

How organisms are spread out across an area — clumped, uniform, or random.

📺 Watch: Population Ecology

🎬 Video 1 — Crash Course Ecology: Population Ecology
Activity 1 Population Growth Classifier STS-K.3

📈 Population Growth

Populations can grow in different patterns depending on resources:

🚀

Exponential Growth

Unlimited resources → population doubles rapidly. Forms a J-curve. This can't last forever!

📊

Logistic Growth

Resources become limited → growth slows and levels off at carrying capacity. Forms an S-curve.

📉

Population Crash

Population exceeds carrying capacity → drastic decline as resources run out. Overshoot and crash!

💡 Key Idea: In nature, most populations follow logistic growth because resources are always limited eventually.

🏔️ Carrying Capacity

Carrying capacity (often called K) is the maximum number of individuals an environment can support long-term. It depends on available food, water, shelter, and space.

When a population overshoots K, resources are consumed faster than they can be replenished. This leads to increased competition, starvation, disease, and a population crash back to or below K.

🚧 Limiting Factors

Limiting factors control how large a population can grow:

Density-Dependent Density-Independent
🦠 Disease spreads faster in crowded populations 🌋 Volcanic eruptions destroy habitat regardless of density
🐺 Predation increases as prey numbers rise 🌊 A flood wipes out all organisms in an area
🍎 Competition for food intensifies with more mouths 🔥 A wildfire destroys an entire forest
🏠 Less shelter as population density increases ❄️ An extreme cold snap affects all organisms

Lesson 4

Human Impact & Stewardship

How human activities affect ecosystems and what we can do to protect them.

🏭 How Humans Change Ecosystems

Humans have a massive impact on ecosystems worldwide. Some changes are intentional, others are unintended consequences.

🪓

Habitat Destruction

Clearing forests, draining wetlands, and building cities removes habitat for countless species.

💨

Pollution

Chemicals, plastic, and waste contaminate water, air, and soil — harming organisms at every trophic level.

🎣

Overuse of Resources

Overfishing, overhunting, and excessive water use deplete resources faster than they regenerate.

🌡️

Climate Change

Burning fossil fuels increases greenhouse gases, disrupting temperature and weather patterns globally.

🐾 Invasive Species & Endangered Species

Invasive species are organisms introduced to new environments where they have no natural predators. They can outcompete native species and disrupt entire ecosystems.

⚠️

Invasive Species

Examples: zebra mussels in the Great Lakes, purple loosestrife in Alberta wetlands, rabbits in Australia.

🦎

Endangered Species

Species at risk of extinction due to habitat loss, pollution, or overexploitation. Alberta examples: woodland caribou, swift fox.

In their native habitat, invasive species have natural predators and competitors that keep their numbers in check. In new environments, they have no natural enemies, so their populations explode, outcompeting native species for food and habitat.

🌱 Conservation & Stewardship

Stewardship means taking responsible care of ecosystems. Everyone can help!

🌲

Protected Areas

National parks, wildlife reserves, and conservation areas protect habitats from development.

♻️

Reduce, Reuse, Recycle

Minimizing waste reduces pollution and conserves resources.

📊

Environmental Monitoring

Scientists track species populations, water quality, and habitat changes to catch problems early.

📜

Laws & Policies

The Species at Risk Act and other laws protect endangered species and their habitats.
