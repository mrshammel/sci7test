🌎 What Is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (plants, animals, fungi, bacteria) interacting with each other and their non-living environment (water, air, soil, sunlight). Ecosystems can be as small as a puddle or as large as the entire Earth!

Every ecosystem has two main types of components working together:

🌱

Biotic Factors

All living or once-living things: plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, and their remains

☀️

Abiotic Factors

All non-living elements: sunlight, water, temperature, soil, air, minerals

💡 Key Idea: Every ecosystem depends on the interactions between biotic and abiotic factors. Remove one piece, and the entire system can be affected!

🔬 Biotic Factors: The Living World

Biotic factors include every living organism in an ecosystem — from the tallest tree to the tiniest bacterium. They can be grouped into:

🌳

Plants

Produce food through photosynthesis; provide oxygen and shelter

🦌

Animals

Consume plants or other animals; help with pollination and seed dispersal

🍄

Fungi

Decompose dead matter; recycle nutrients back into the soil

🦠

Bacteria

Break down organic materials; some fix nitrogen for plant use

Dead organisms are still classified as biotic because they were once living. Fallen leaves, dead animals, and decaying wood all still play crucial roles in the ecosystem — decomposers break them down, returning nutrients to the soil that living plants can use. The distinction is about origin, not current state!

💧 Abiotic Factors: The Non-Living World

Abiotic factors are the non-living parts of an ecosystem. Even though they aren't alive, they are essential for the survival of every organism.

Abiotic Factor Role in the Ecosystem
☀️ Sunlight Energy source for photosynthesis; affects temperature and seasons
💧 Water Essential for all life; habitat for aquatic organisms
🌡️ Temperature Determines which species can survive in an area
🪨 Soil & Minerals Provides nutrients for plants; habitat for organisms like worms
💨 Air (Atmosphere) Provides oxygen for animals and CO₂ for plants
🌧️ Precipitation Delivers water to land ecosystems; shapes landscapes

🔗 How Ecosystems Meet the Needs of Life

Every living organism needs certain things to survive. A healthy ecosystem provides all of these:

🥗

Nutrients

Food and minerals for growth, energy, and repair

⚡

Energy

Sunlight drives photosynthesis; energy flows through food chains

💦

Moisture

Water for cellular processes, temperature regulation

🏠

Habitat

Shelter, space, and suitable conditions for living and reproducing

🌬️

Gas Exchange

O₂ for animals, CO₂ for plants — a constant cycle

💡 Alberta Outcome: Illustrate how life-supporting environments meet the needs of living things for nutrients, energy sources, moisture, suitable habitat, and exchange of gases. (STS-K.1)

Food Chains, Food Webs & Energy Flow

  • Understand energy pyramids and why energy decreases at each level

⚡ Energy in Ecosystems

All life needs energy to survive. In nearly every ecosystem on Earth, that energy begins with the Sun. But how does energy get from sunlight to a wolf? Through a series of steps called a food chain!

Energy flows in one direction: from the Sun → to producers → to consumers → and is recycled by decomposers.

💡 Key Idea: Energy is not recycled — it flows one way and is lost as heat at each step. Only about 10% of energy is passed from one level to the next!

🌱 Producers, Consumers & Decomposers

Every organism in an ecosystem plays a role based on how it gets its energy:

🌿

Producers

Autotrophs — make their own food through photosynthesis. Examples: grass, trees, algae

🐰

Primary Consumers

Herbivores — eat producers. Examples: rabbits, deer, grasshoppers

🐍

Secondary Consumers

Carnivores/Omnivores — eat primary consumers. Examples: snakes, frogs

🦅

Tertiary Consumers

Top predators — eat secondary consumers. Examples: eagles, wolves, sharks

🍄

Decomposers

Break down dead organisms and waste, returning nutrients to soil. Examples: fungi, bacteria

Scavengers (like vultures and hyenas) eat dead animals but don't break them down completely. Decomposers (like bacteria and fungi) break down dead matter at the molecular level, returning nutrients directly to the soil.

🔗 Food Chains

A food chain is a simple, straight-line path showing who eats whom. Each step is called a trophic level.

🌞 Sun → 🌱 Grass (producer) → 🐰 Rabbit (primary consumer) → 🐍 Snake (secondary consumer) → 🦅 Hawk (tertiary consumer) → 🍄 Decomposers

The arrows in a food chain show the direction that energy flows.

🕸️ Food Webs

In reality, most organisms eat more than one thing and are eaten by more than one predator. A food web shows all of these interconnected food chains.

Removing one species can have a ripple effect throughout the entire food web! This is called a trophic cascade. The more interconnected a food web is, the more stable the ecosystem tends to be.

🔺 Energy Pyramids

An energy pyramid shows how much energy is available at each trophic level.

🦅 Tertiary
1 kcal
🐍 Secondary
10 kcal
🐰 Primary
100 kcal
🌿 Producers
1,000 kcal

At each level, about 90% of the energy is lost as heat. Only ~10% is passed on. This is the 10% Rule.
