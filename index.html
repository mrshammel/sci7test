Earth's Layers, Rocks & Minerals

Discover what's inside the Earth, how scientists study its layers, and how rocks are classified using the rock cycle.

7-E.17-E.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can describe the layers of Earth and explain what each layer is made of.
  • I can explain how scientists study Earth's interior without seeing it directly.
  • I can classify rocks as igneous, sedimentary, or metamorphic and explain how each type forms.
  • I can describe the rock cycle and how rocks change from one type to another.

✅ Success Criteria

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to check off all of these:

  • I can name and order Earth's 4 main layers from surface to centre.
  • I can describe at least one way scientists learn about Earth's interior.
  • I can tell the difference between a rock and a mineral.
  • I can identify the 3 rock types and explain how each forms.
  • I can draw or describe the rock cycle showing how rocks change.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Hover over each term to see its definition. You'll encounter these throughout the lesson.

🪨 MineralMineral: A naturally occurring, non-living solid with a specific chemical makeup and crystal structure. Example: quartz, feldspar. 🏔️ RockRock: A solid made of one or more minerals. Rocks are classified by how they form. 🌋 IgneousIgneous Rock: Formed when molten rock (magma/lava) cools and hardens. Example: granite, basalt. 🏖️ SedimentarySedimentary Rock: Formed when small particles (sediment) are compressed and cemented together over time. Example: sandstone, limestone. ♨️ MetamorphicMetamorphic Rock: Formed when existing rocks are changed by extreme heat and pressure deep underground. Example: marble, slate. 🔄 Rock CycleRock Cycle: The continuous process by which rocks change from one type to another through melting, cooling, weathering, erosion, compaction, and heat/pressure. 🌍 CrustCrust: The thin, solid outermost layer of Earth. Includes continental (thicker) and oceanic (thinner) crust. 🔥 MantleMantle: The thick, hot layer beneath the crust. Made of semi-solid rock that flows very slowly.

🌍 What's Inside Earth?

If you could slice Earth in half like an apple, you'd see it's made of distinct layers — like a boiled egg with a shell, white, and yolk. Scientists have never drilled to the centre, but they've figured out what's down there using seismic waves from earthquakes.

🥚

Crust

5–70 km thick. The thin, rocky outer layer we live on. Thinnest under oceans, thickest under mountains like the Canadian Rockies.

🔥

Mantle

~2,900 km thick. Hot, semi-solid rock that flows slowly. Convection currents here drive plate movement.

💧

Outer Core

~2,200 km thick. Liquid iron and nickel. Creates Earth's magnetic field that protects us from solar radiation.

⚡

Inner Core

~1,200 km radius. Solid iron and nickel — incredibly hot (~5,400°C) but kept solid by extreme pressure.

💡 Alberta Connection: The Canadian Rockies formed when tectonic plates collided, pushing crust upward. The rocks in Banff and Jasper contain fossils of ancient sea creatures — evidence that this land was once underwater!

🪨 Three Types of Rock

Every rock on Earth falls into one of three categories based on how it was formed.

🌋

Igneous

Formed from cooled magma/lava. Fast cooling = small crystals (basalt). Slow cooling = large crystals (granite).

🏖️

Sedimentary

Formed from compressed layers of sediment. Often contains fossils. Examples: sandstone, limestone, shale.

♨️

Metamorphic

Changed by heat & pressure. Limestone → marble. Shale → slate. Granite → gneiss.

💡 Alberta Connection: The hoodoos near Drumheller are made of sedimentary rock — layers of sand, silt, and clay deposited millions of years ago. Dinosaur bones found there are preserved in these layers!

Igneous: Look for crystals or glassy texture. No layers. Sedimentary: Look for visible layers, fossils, or grains. Often crumbly. Metamorphic: Look for banding, folding, or a shiny/wavy appearance. Very hard.

⏸️ Pause and Check

  • A rock contains a fossil. What type of rock is it most likely?
  • What's the difference between a rock and a mineral?
  • If granite is put under extreme heat and pressure underground, what type of rock does it become?

Answers: Sedimentary · A mineral is a single substance; a rock is made of minerals · Metamorphic (gneiss)

🔄 The Rock Cycle

Rocks don't stay the same forever! Over millions of years, rocks are constantly changing from one type to another. This continuous process is called the rock cycle.

The Rock Cycle — Key Processes

🌋

Melting → Cooling

Any rock can melt into magma. When magma cools, it forms igneous rock.

🌧️

Weathering → Compaction

Rocks break down into sediment. Layers compress over time into sedimentary rock.

♨️

Heat + Pressure

Any rock deep underground can be changed by heat and pressure into metamorphic rock.

💡 The rock cycle has no beginning or end — rocks can enter the cycle at any point and change into any other type.

Plate Tectonics & Geological Change

Discover how Earth’s plates move, why earthquakes and volcanoes happen, and how mountains are built.

7-E.37-E.4

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can explain the theory of plate tectonics and what drives plate movement.
  • I can describe the 3 types of plate boundaries and what happens at each.
  • I can explain how earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountains are related to plate boundaries.
  • I can describe evidence that supports plate tectonics (continental drift).

✅ Success Criteria

  • I can sort examples into convergent, divergent, or transform boundaries.
  • I can match geological events to their plate boundary types.
  • I can explain Wegener’s evidence for continental drift.
  • I can predict what will happen when two plates converge vs. diverge.

📖 Key Vocabulary

🌍 Tectonic PlateTectonic Plate: A massive slab of Earth’s lithosphere that floats on the semi-fluid asthenosphere. There are about 15 major plates. ⬆️ ConvergentConvergent Boundary: Where plates push toward each other. Can form mountains, volcanoes, or ocean trenches. ⬇️ DivergentDivergent Boundary: Where plates pull apart. Magma rises to fill the gap, creating new crust (e.g., mid-ocean ridges). ↔️ TransformTransform Boundary: Where plates slide past each other. Causes earthquakes (e.g., San Andreas Fault). 🌋 SubductionSubduction: When a denser plate dives under a lighter plate at a convergent boundary, often causing volcanoes. 🌀 Convection CurrentConvection Current: Circular flow in the mantle caused by heat from the core — the driving force behind plate movement.

🌍 What Are Tectonic Plates?

Earth’s outer shell (the lithosphere) is broken into about 15 large pieces called tectonic plates. These plates float on the hot, slowly-flowing mantle below. They move only a few centimetres per year — about as fast as your fingernails grow!

💡 Key Idea: Plates move because of convection currents in the mantle. Hot rock near the core rises, cools near the surface, and sinks back down — this circular flow drags the plates along.

🗺️ Three Types of Plate Boundaries

⬆️

Convergent

Plates push together → mountains, volcanoes, ocean trenches, subduction zones

⬇️

Divergent

Plates pull apart → new crust forms, mid-ocean ridges, rift valleys

↔️

Transform

Plates slide past each other → earthquakes along fault lines

⏸️ Pause and Check

  • What drives the movement of tectonic plates?
  • What happens at a convergent boundary?
  • How fast do plates typically move?

Answers: Convection currents in the mantle · Plates push together — can form mountains, volcanoes, or subduction zones · A few centimetres per year (about fingernail growth speed)

🌋 Earthquakes & Volcanoes

Most earthquakes and volcanoes occur at plate boundaries. The “Ring of Fire” around the Pacific Ocean has about 75% of the world’s volcanoes and 90% of its earthquakes!

EventBoundary TypeWhat Happens
🌋 Volcanic EruptionConvergent (subduction)Subducting plate melts → magma rises → volcano
🌊 EarthquakeAll three, especially transformPlates grind, stick, then suddenly slip
⛰️ Mountain BuildingConvergentTwo continental plates collide and crumple upward

💡 Alberta Connection: The Canadian Rocky Mountains formed millions of years ago when the Pacific and North American plates collided. The immense pressure crumpled Earth’s crust upward, creating the spectacular peaks we see across western Alberta and British Columbia today. Banff and Jasper sit on these ancient collision zones!

🧩 Evidence for Continental Drift

In 1912, Alfred Wegener proposed that continents were once joined in a supercontinent called Pangaea. His evidence:

🧩

Puzzle Fit

Continents fit together like puzzle pieces (Africa + South America)

🦕

Matching Fossils

Same fossil species found on continents now separated by oceans

🪨

Rock Matches

Same rock types and mountain chains line up across continents

❄️

Glacial Evidence

Glacial scratches found in now-tropical areas that were once near the poles

⏸️ Pause and Check

  • What was Pangaea?
  • Name 3 pieces of evidence Wegener used.
  • How did the Rocky Mountains form?

Answers: A supercontinent where all landmasses were joined together · Puzzle fit of continents, matching fossils, matching rock types · Plate collision (convergent boundary) crumpled the crust upward

📖 Key Vocabulary

Rock Cycle: The continuous process of rocks transforming between igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic types
Magma/Lava: Molten rock — underground = magma, on surface = lava. Cools to form igneous rock.

Weathering, Erosion & Sedimentation

Learn how wind, water, ice, and chemical reactions break down rocks and reshape Earth's surface — including Alberta's iconic landforms.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Mechanical Weathering: Physical breakdown of rock — freeze-thaw, root growth, abrasion
Chemical Weathering: Dissolving or altering minerals in rock — acid rain, oxidation, carbonation
Erosion: Transport of broken rock by wind, water, ice, or gravity
Sedimentation: When transported sediment settles and accumulates in layers

Fossils & the Geological Time Scale

Use fossils to read Earth's history and understand the immense scale of geological time — including Alberta's world-class fossil sites.

E.K.1E.K.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can explain how fossils form in sedimentary rock.
  • I can use fossils to determine the relative age of rock layers.
  • I can describe Alberta's significance in fossil science (Drumheller, Royal Tyrrell Museum).

📖 Key Vocabulary

Fossil: Preserved remains or traces of ancient life — bones, shells, footprints, leaf imprints
Relative Dating: Determining which rock layers are older or younger by position — deeper = older
Geological Time Scale: Timeline dividing Earth's 4.6 billion year history into eons, eras, periods, and epochs
Index Fossil: Fossils from organisms that lived for a short time over a wide area — useful for dating rock layers
